Need for a Post Quantum Cryptography resistant infrastructure

The Threat is Real and Imminent

Quantum computers will soon be powerful enough to break the encryption that protects our data. This isn't a distant problem; the risk of "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" attacks is already here.

2029

The Year Gartner Predicts Current Crypto Becomes Vulnerable

This is the critical window. Data encrypted today with a lifespan beyond 2029 is at immediate risk of being harvested by adversaries for future decryption. (Source: Gartner)

The Solution: NIST's PQC Standards

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has finalized the first set of quantum-resistant cryptographic algorithms. Our migration will focus on adopting these standards throughout our infrastructure.

ML-KEM

(FIPS 203)

The primary standard for general-purpose encryption, replacing methods like RSA and ECC for key exchange. (Source: NIST)

ML-DSA

(FIPS 204)

The primary standard for digital signatures, ensuring data integrity and authenticity in a post-quantum world. (Source: NIST)

SLH-DSA

(FIPS 205)

A backup digital signature standard based on a different mathematical approach for enhanced security diversity. (Source: NIST)

Decision Factors

Risks in choosing to delay migration

  • Catastrophic Security Vulnerability: Sensitive data, communications, and systems vulnerable to "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks.
  • Significant Financial Penalties & Legal Liabilities: Costly regulatory fines and lawsuits.
  • Compliance Failure: Inability to operate in certain markets or with key partners due to non-compliance.
  • More Complex and Costly Reactive Remediation: Forced, chaotic, and expensive recovery after a breach.
  • Increased Operational Disruption: Unplanned outages and business interruptions.
